Time-Series Forecasting U.S. Exports to Canada

Prediction of Trade Volume

Author

Erick Xavier Maldonado

1. Business Understanding

The client is a large U.S. Manufacturing firm that exports a significant volume of goods to Canada. The firm faces challenges in supply chain planning due to fluctuations in international demand. Accurate forecasting of export volumes is critical for:

  • Inventory Management: Ensuring raw materials are available to meet production schedules without overstocking.

  • Logistics Planning: Securing freight capacity (trucking/rail) in advance to avoid spot-market premiums.

Objective: The goal is to forecast the value of U.S. Exports to Canada (EXPCA) for the upcoming 6 months (Sept 2025 – Feb 2026).

Research Questions: To guide this analysis, I propose the following research questions:

  • To what extent does the inclusion of a structural break variable, representing the COVID-19 shock, improve the predictive accuracy of linear regression models for U.S.-Canada trade?

  • Does a short-term exponential smoothing model (focused on the post-2022 recovery) provide a more risk-averse forecast for inventory management compared to a long-term trend regression?

Methodology: I have restricted our analysis to the Post-Financial Crisis era (2010–2025). This excludes the structural volatility of the pre-2008 economy, ensuring the model’s trends reflect modern supply chain dynamics. I will compare:

  • Regression Analysis: Incorporating trend, seasonality, and a dummy variable to account for the COVID-19 structural shock.

  • Exponential Smoothing (ETS): Focusing on the post-shock recovery period to capture the most recent market dynamics.

The model with the lowest error (RMSE/MAPE) and highest stability will be selected for deployment.

3. Modeling with Regression Approach

Step 3.1

Structural Break Analysis

We analyze the series for structural breaks to confirm the impact of COVID-19. However, per client instructions, we will retain the pre-COVID history and instead manage the disruption using an exogenous variable.

Identified Structural Breakpoints
Break_Date Date_Label

The Bai-Perron test confirms that even when restricting the analysis to the post-2010 period, the trade series is not static. The test identifies significant breakpoints at 2012(4), 2014(12), 2017(4), and most critically, 2021(9).

The break at September 2021 is particularly relevant as it marks the transition from the immediate COVID shock to the recovery phase. This validates our modeling strategy I wanted to apply while the long-term trend is useful, the structural shifts mean we must account for the specific volatility of the COVID era to avoid distorting our future predictions.

Step 3.2

COVID Binary Variable

We create a dummy variable. We assumed that the primary COVID shock on trade logistics lasted from March 2020 through December 2021, encompassing border restrictions and initial supply chain crunches.

Step 3.3

We compare two regression specifications:

  • Model A (Standard): Exports ~ Trend + Season

  • Model B (Shock-Adjusted): Exports ~ Trend + Season + CovidDummy

Validation Strategy (Rolling Origin):

To ensure the model predicts “unseen” future data accurately, we used a rolling origin cross-validation strategy:

  • Fold 1: Train (Jan 2010 – Dec 2022), Validate on 2023 (12 months).

  • Fold 2: Train (Jan 2010 – Dec 2023), Validate on 2024 (12 months).

We evaluated the model’s performance by forecasting “unseen” data for 2023 and 2024. The results revealed a significant divergence between the two approaches:

  • Model A (No Dummy): Produced an Average RMSE of 2653.36 and an Average MAPE of 8.55%.
  • Model B (With Dummy): Produced a significantly lower Average RMSE of 1522.75 and an Average MAPE of 4.50%.

Unlike the initial hypothesis, the “With Dummy” model performed drastically better in the cross-validation. This indicates that the COVID-19 shock (2020–2021) was not just a temporary noise event but a structural distortion that still impacts how the model reads the trend. By explicitly accounting for the shock with a dummy variable, the regression model could more accurately predict the stable behavior of 2023 and 2024.

Modeling with Smoothing Approach

Step 4.1: Period Selection

Even with our shorter 2010 baseline, the COVID shock (2020) is a massive distortion for smoothing weights. We continue to use the Post-COVID Recovery Period (Jan 2022 – Aug 2025) to ensure the model is hyper-focused on the “New Normal.”

When restricting the view to the post-COVID recovery period (2022–2025), the ets() algorithm selected an ETS(M,N,M) model.

  • Error (M): Multiplicative, appropriate for the high-variance data.

  • Trend (N): None. This is the critical differentiator. While the regression model sees a 15-year upward trend, the smoothing model looks at the last 3 years and sees no consistent growth. It interprets the recent high export values as a plateau rather than a climb.

  • Seasonality (M): Multiplicative seasonality remains a strong driver.

The model’s Alpha (0.36) indicates it is moderately responsive to new data, quickly adapting its baseline without reacting wildly to every single month’s fluctuation.

Selected Method: Exponential Smoothing (ETS)

We compared two distinct narratives:

  • Regression (Optimistic): Predicts continued growth based on a 15-year trend (Avg Forecast ~$28B).

  • ETS (Conservative): Predicts a stable/declining plateau based on recent stagnation (Avg Forecast ~$26.5B).

Metrics: The Regression model (With Dummy) achieved a validation MAPE of 4.50%. The ETS model achieved an in-sample MAPE of 2.77%. Both models are statistically valid.

Rationale:

  • Risk Profile: The client (U.S. Manufacturer) specifically flagged overstocking as a critical risk. The Regression model is optimistic, if we follow it and demand stays flat, we will overproduce by ~$2B. The ETS model is conservative since it protects against overstocking.

  • Recency: The ETS model’s “No Trend” finding accurately reflects the visible stagnation in the data since 2022, making it the safer bet for immediate planning.

6. Deployment

Business Guidance for the U.S. Manufacturer:

  1. Inventory Strategy (Conservative Baseline): Prepare for a softening in demand. The forecast predicts volume will contract from 27.3B in October down to 24.6B in January 2026. Align raw material purchases to this lower baseline to avoid carrying excess inventory into the new year.

  2. Handling the Temporary Blip: While the model interprets recent dips as a plateau, we acknowledge the potential for a rebound. To balance the conservative forecast against the risk of stock outs:

  • Maintain Capacity: Do not reduce workforce or trucking contracts permanently.

  • Safety Stock: Implement a 10% Safety Stock buffer which is a approx. $2.5B inventory equivalent. This allows you to fulfill orders if demand surprises to the upside (towards the Regression forecast) without committing to full-scale overproduction.

  1. Logistics Execution: October 2025 is projected to be the peak month ($27.35B). Secure freight capacity for this specific month immediately. Also, plan for reduced shipping volume in January/February 2026, allowing for maintenance downtime or lower spot-market spending.
